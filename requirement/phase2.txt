Overview
In Phase 1, you predicted masked neural activity from partial observations. In Phase 2, the task flips: decode finger positions from neural activity — with missing channels and neural drift.

Intracortical brain-computer interfaces (iBCIs) must translate neural signals into movement commands. Two fundamental challenges are neural drift — signal statistics shift over days, weeks, and months — and channel dropout — electrodes degrade or fail over time. A robust decoder must handle both.

In this competition, you will work with long-term intracortical recordings spanning hundreds of sessions collected over multiple years from a non-human primate performing a finger-pressing task. Your goal is to predict 2 finger position variables from 96-channel Spiking Band Power (SBP) at each time bin across 125 held-out test sessions.

Key challenges:

Neural drift: Training sessions span 60% days ; test sessions span the rest. Your decoder must generalize across months of signal drift.
Channel dropout: ~30% of channels are randomly zeroed out in each session (simulating electrode degradation). Your model must be robust to missing input channels.

Description
The Data
The dataset consists of recordings from a 96-channel intracortical electrode array (two 64-channel Utah arrays) implanted in the motor cortex of a non-human primate. During each recording session, the subject performed a finger-movement task while neural activity and finger kinematics were captured simultaneously.

Each session provides:

Spiking Band Power (SBP): A 96-channel neural feature extracted at 50 Hz (20 ms bins). Shape: (N_timebins, 96). ~30% of channels are zeroed out per session (different channels each session), simulating real-world electrode degradation.
Finger Kinematics (training only): 4 behavioral variables — index finger position, middle-ring-pinky (MRP) finger position, index velocity, and MRP velocity. Positions are normalized to [0, 1]. Shape: (N_timebins, 4).
Trial Information: Each session contains multiple trials (~375 per session). Trial start/end time bins are provided.
Note: While the kinematics files contain 4 columns (positions and velocities), only the 2 position channels are evaluated. Velocity is provided for convenience — it is simply the discrete time derivative of position (vel[t] = pos[t+1] - pos[t]).

Target Variables
Column	Description
index_pos	Index finger position (0 = extended, 1 = flexed), range [0, 1]
mrp_pos	Middle-ring-pinky finger position (0 = extended, 1 = flexed), range [0, 1]
Channel Dropout
~30% of channels (28 out of 96) are randomly zeroed out in every session. The zeroed channels differ per session. You can identify which channels are active by checking which columns are all-zero:

sbp = np.load('train/D042_sbp.npy')        # (N, 96)
active_mask = ~(sbp == 0).all(axis=0)       # True for active channels
print(f"Active channels: {active_mask.sum()}/96")  # ~68/96
Your model must handle varying subsets of active channels across sessions.

Decoding Approaches
There are two natural strategies for predicting finger position:

Direct position decoding: Predict position directly from neural activity.
Velocity-first decoding: Decode velocity from neural activity, then integrate to recover position (since position = integral of velocity). This can be more natural because velocity is zero-anchored, while position is relative to an arbitrary reference point. If you take this approach, make sure to calibrate your integrated output so the final position values are on the correct scale.
The Task
For each of the 125 test sessions, you are given SBP neural activity (with ~30% channels zeroed) but kinematics are withheld. Your goal is to predict the 2 finger position variables at every time bin.

Your goal: predict finger positions from neural activity at each time bin.

This is a supervised decoding task. You have 187 training sessions with paired SBP and kinematics to learn the neural-to-kinematic mapping, then must apply your decoder to 125 test sessions where only SBP is available.

The split is chronological: training sessions come from the first ~60% of the recording timeline, and test sessions from the last ~40%. This means test sessions are always in the future relative to training — your decoder must handle neural drift without access to future calibration data.

Evaluation
Submissions are evaluated by comparing your predicted positions to the hidden ground truth at all time bins across 125 test sessions (~3.0M total time bins).

R² (Coefficient of Determination)
R² measures how well your predictions capture the variance in the true positions. It is computed per session, per position channel:

                         sum((predicted_i - true_i)^2)
R²(s, c) = 1  -  -----------------------------------------
                   sum((true_i - mean(true))^2)
Where:

s = session, c = position channel
mean(true) = mean of the true values for channel c in session s
The sums are over all time bins in the session Final Score:
R² = mean of R²(s, c) across all 125 sessions x 2 channels = 250 groups
This ensures all sessions and channels contribute equally regardless of signal magnitude. Range: 1.0 and below (higher is better). On the Kaggle leaderboard, a higher R² corresponds to a higher rank.

R²	Meaning
1.0	Perfect prediction
> 0.0	Model captures meaningful position variance
0.0	Equivalent to predicting the per-session channel mean (trivial baseline)
< 0.0	Worse than the trivial baseline

Public leaderboard: 64 test sessions
Private leaderboard: 61 test sessions The split uses blockwise allocation along the recording timeline. The final ranking uses the private portion. This prevents overfitting to the leaderboard. Baseline model scores (on masked data with 30% channel dropout):
